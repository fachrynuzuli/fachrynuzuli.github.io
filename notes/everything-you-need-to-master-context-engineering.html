Everything You Need to Master Context Engineering

Providing context to an AI model is literally the difference maker between getting outputs that are actually personalized to your situation versus getting generic AI slop that sound generic af.

When you don't give an AI any context about your business, your voice, or what you're trying to accomplish, it just guesses.

But when you feed it the right information, everything changes. Your outputs start sounding like you and actually solve your real problems.

And obviously as a result, context is honestly one of the biggest factors when implementing AI systems.

For me most of the time spent on a system design is focused on how do we get the right context at the right time based on the user’s input

And also most of prompt engineering is really just learning how to provide context effectively.

Static vs Dynamic Context

Let's use a content generation system as an example to break this down.

Say you have a system where someone puts in a headline and some context about what they want to write about, and it spits out a Twitter thread.

There are two types of context you need to think about: static and dynamic.

Static context is stuff that never changes no matter what the user asks for. This includes things like your brand voice guidelines, audience research, copywriting frameworks

Basically anything that should stay consistent across every piece of content you create.

Dynamic context changes based on what the user is actually asking for.

Here's where most people mess up: they treat dynamic context as static context and dumping everything they have into the prompt on every run.

So if someone wants to write a thread about losing weight, they'll paste in 30 fitness threads as "examples" but 90% of them are semi related but not directly related

What you actually want to do is provide 3-4 threads that performed well in the past and are actually similar to what they're trying to write. If they want to write about losing weight, give the AI examples of your best-performing threads about losing weight, not random threads about lifting heavy or back pain.

The key with dynamic context is you want to give the AI the most relevant examples based on the specific input that’s being made.

Another example is is for a client support bot

You don’t want to give it all your SOPs, call transcripts, slack history that’s just too much shit

If the question is about onboarding, the things I’d give the LLM would be the SOP for onboarding, similar questions clients have asked in the past and our answer along with any relevant info on the client itself.

And why this is dynamic context is because this will change based on if a different client is asking this question (as client info will change) and will change if a client asks a different type of question like “how do upgrade to the enterprise plan” would require a different SOP and different set of example questions.

So providing static context is quite simple as you can hard code that into a prompt so the rest of this doc will be focused on how to incorporate dynamic context into an AI system

Embeddings and RAG

An embedding is just a fancy way of turning a chunk of text into numbers. Specifically, a long list of numbers that represents the meaning of that text.

Think of it like this: humans understand that "How do I change my credit card?" and "How can I update my payment info?" mean the same thing even though the words are different.

But computers don't naturally understand language the way we do. They need a way to "see" that these sentences have the same meaning.

That's where embeddings come in.

When you run text through an embedding model, it converts it into a list of numbers:

"How do I change my credit card?" \= \[0.25, \-0.60, 0.34, 0.18\]

"How can I update my payment info?" \= \[0.24, \-0.62, 0.30, 0.21\]

In the real world, these lists are thousands of numbers long, but for this example, notice how similar the numbers are? That's because the sentences mean the same thing.

Now here's the key part: since similar meanings produce similar numbers, we can use math to find text that means the same thing.

If someone asks "How do I update my billing info?" the system can:

1. Turn that question into numbers
2. Compare it to all the other numbered text in your content related to billing, shipping, marketing, etc.
3. Find the texts with the most similar numbers (which will be context related to billing)
4. Pull up the most relevant information to answer the question

This is how AI systems automatically find the right context without you having to manually search through everything yourself.

Instead of the AI guessing or giving generic answers, it can pull exactly the right information based on what the user is actually asking for.

This whole process using embeddings to find and retrieve relevant information — is called RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation).

It's basically the standard way most AI chatbots and internal tools retrieve dynamic context that's relevant to the user input.

Going back to the content generation example:

Let's say you have 200 Twitter threads you've written over the past year. Here's how this would work:

Step 1: You embed each 200 of your past threads and store them (this is done once upfront)
Step 2: User inputs a headline like "5 ways to lose weight without going to the gym"
Step 3: The system turns that headline into an embedding
Step 4: It compares that headline embedding to all 200 of your pre-embedded threads
Step 5: It finds the 3 threads with the most similar embeddings (which be threads about weight loss)
Step 6: Those 3 relevant threads get injected as examples for the AI to learn from when writing the new thread

So instead of you manually digging through 200 threads to find relevant examples, or just dumping random threads as context, the system automatically pulls the most relevant ones based on what you're trying to write about.

Categorization Approach

With retrieving dynamic context using embeddings, it will grab you the most similar content based on meaning, which is usually good.

But sometimes it can mess up \- like if a customer sends: "My package never arrived and I need it ASAP because it's my sister's birthday gift and I wanted to surprise her with something special since she's been going through a tough time lately and birthdays are really important to our family..."

The embedding search might get confused by words like "gift," "birthday," and "special" and pull in context about gift wrapping services or birthday promotions instead of the shipping/lost package information they actually need.

But with advancements in AI models and larger context windows, sometimes retrieval isn't necessary at all.

For example, let's say you have a customer support bot and someone makes an inquiry that they didn't get their package. Yeah, ideally we'd use embeddings to find the specific snippet on how we handle lost packages. But since we know this is a shipping problem, we could just provide our entire shipping documentation as context.

For customer support, most docs for each topic aren't extremely long, so we can just provide the whole doc and get a much higher accuracy rate. And when I say accuracy, I mean providing relevant context that's actually needed to answer the question.

Instead of having to find the exact piece of text in our entire documentation, the game just becomes: what topic does this question cover?

Here's how to do it:

Step 1: Give the LLM the user input along with a predefined list of categories (shipping, billing, refunds, account management, etc.)

Step 2: Have the LLM tag which category the question falls under

Step 3: Use a simple if/else statement \- if it's tagged as "shipping," inject the shipping docs into the prompt

The key is having these categories predefined. If you don't do that, you risk formatting issues and inconsistent tagging. This way, you get more reliable context retrieval for structured use cases.

When to use categorization vs embedding similarity search:

Use categorization when you have clear, distinct topics and each category has a manageable amount of content (like customer support with shipping, billing, refunds docs).

Use embedding similarity search when you need to find specific, nuanced matches from a large amount of content:

- finding the 3 most relevant threads from 200+ past threads based on topic and style)
- You have a massive operations manual or company handbook that covers dozens of different processes all in one document, so you break it into chunks and let the system find the exact section that matches the user's question

Providing Relevant Training Data

This method is different from RAG or categorization because instead of just providing reference content, you're giving the AI examples of transformations with explanations.

Let's go back to the content generation example. A lot of content agencies have told me: "Hey, we have all these content reviews where our team gives us a thread, and then we reformat it with feedback on why the new version is better."

So you have:

* Original thread (before)
* Improved thread (after)
* Explanation of why the new version is better

This is incredibly valuable training data.

Here's how to use it:

Step 1: Take each content review (original thread \+ improved version \+ feedback) and embed just the original thread

Step 2: Store all these embeddings along with their corresponding improved versions and feedback

Step 3: When someone submits a headline and context for generating a new thread, the system finds similar original threads from your past reviews that relate to the topic they want to write about

Step 4: It provides the AI with those similar before/after examples and feedback, so when generating the new thread, it understands the specific nuances of what you're looking for when writing threads on this topic

This teaches the AI the specific nuances of what you're looking for and how you like things done.

This method works when you have a lot of before/after examples with feedback.

So you could use this for:

* Sales call reviews: embed objection handling sections from past calls with your feedback on what could be improved
* Email reviews: before/after examples of cold emails with notes on why certain changes were made
* Client proposal reviews: past proposals with your edits and reasoning

The key is having a substantial amount of transformation examples so the AI can learn your specific standards and preferences.
