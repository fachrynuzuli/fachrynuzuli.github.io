Everything You Need to Master Context Engineering

Providing context to an AI model is literally the difference maker between getting outputs that are actually personalized to your situation versus getting generic AI slop that sound generic af.

When you don't give an AI any context about your business, your voice, or what you're trying to accomplish, it just guesses.

But when you feed it the right information, everything changes. Your outputs start sounding like you and actually solve your real problems.

And obviously as a result, context is honestly one of the biggest factors when implementing AI systems.

For me most of the time spent on a system design is focused on how do we get the right context at the right time based on the user’s input

And also most of prompt engineering is really just learning how to provide context effectively.

Static vs Dynamic Context

Let's use a content generation system as an example to break this down.

Say you have a system where someone puts in a headline and some context about what they want to write about, and it spits out a Twitter thread.

There are two types of context you need to think about: static and dynamic.

Static context is stuff that never changes no matter what the user asks for. This includes things like your brand voice guidelines, audience research, copywriting frameworks

Basically anything that should stay consistent across every piece of content you create.

Dynamic context changes based on what the user is actually asking for.

Here's where most people mess up: they treat dynamic context as static context and dumping everything they have into the prompt on every run.

So if someone wants to write a thread about losing weight, they'll paste in 30 fitness threads as "examples" but 90% of them are semi related but not directly related

What you actually want to do is provide 3-4 threads that performed well in the past and are actually similar to what they're trying to write. If they want to write about losing weight, give the AI examples of your best-performing threads about losing weight, not random threads about lifting heavy or back pain.

The key with dynamic context is you want to give the AI the most relevant examples based on the specific input that’s being made.

Another example is is for a client support bot

You don’t want to give it all your SOPs, call transcripts, slack history that’s just too much shit

If the question is about onboarding, the things I’d give the LLM would be the SOP for onboarding, similar questions clients have asked in the past and our answer along with any relevant info on the client itself.

And why this is dynamic context is because this will change based on if a different client is asking this question (as client info will change) and will change if a client asks a different type of question like “how do upgrade to the enterprise plan” would require a different SOP and different set of example questions.

So providing static context is quite simple as you can hard code that into a prompt so the rest of this doc will be focused on how to incorporate dynamic context into an AI system

Embeddings and RAG

An embedding is just a fancy way of turning a chunk of text into numbers. Specifically, a long list of numbers that represents the meaning of that text.

Think of it like this: humans understand that "How do I change my credit card?" and "How can I update my payment info?" mean the same thing even though the words are different.

But computers don't naturally understand language the way we do. They need a way to "see" that these sentences have the same meaning.

That's where embeddings come in.

When you run text through an embedding model, it converts it into a list of numbers:

"How do I change my credit card?" \= \[0.25, \-0.60, 0.34, 0.18\]

"How can I update my payment info?" \= \[0.24, \-0.62, 0.30, 0.21\]

In the real world, these lists are thousands of numbers long, but for this example, notice how similar the numbers are? That's because the sentences mean the same thing.

Now here's the key part: since similar meanings produce similar numbers, we can use math to find text that means the same thing.

If someone asks "How do I update my billing info?" the system can:

1. Turn that question into numbers
2. Compare it to all the other numbered text in your content related to billing, shipping, marketing, etc.
3. Find the texts with the most similar numbers (which will be context related to billing)
4. Pull up the most relevant information to answer the question

This is how AI systems automatically find the right context without you having to manually search through everything yourself.

Instead of the AI guessing or giving generic answers, it can pull exactly the right information based on what the user is actually asking for.

This whole process using embeddings to find and retrieve relevant information — is called RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation).

It's basically the standard way most AI chatbots and internal tools retrieve dynamic context that's relevant to the user input.

Going back to the content generation example:

Let's say you have 200 Twitter threads you've written over the past year. Here's how this would work:

Step 1: You embed each 200 of your past threads and store them (this is done once upfront)
Step 2: User inputs a headline like "5 ways to lose weight without going to the gym"
Step 3: The system turns that headline into an embedding
Step 4: It compares that headline embedding to all 200 of your pre-embedded threads
Step 5: It finds the 3 threads with the most similar embeddings (which be threads about weight loss)
Step 6: Those 3 relevant threads get injected as examples for the AI to learn from when writing the new thread

So instead of you manually digging through 200 threads to find relevant examples, or just dumping random threads as context, the system automatically pulls the most relevant ones based on what you're trying to write about.

Categorization Approach

With retrieving dynamic context using embeddings, it will grab you the most similar content based on meaning, which is usually good.

But sometimes it can mess up \- like if a customer sends: "My package never arrived and I need it ASAP because it's my sister's birthday gift and I wanted to surprise her with something special since she's been going through a tough time lately and birthdays are really important to our family..."

The embedding search might get confused by words like "gift," "birthday," and "special" and pull in context about gift wrapping services or birthday promotions instead of the shipping/lost package information they actually need.

But with advancements in AI models and larger context windows, sometimes retrieval isn't necessary at all.

For example, let's say you have a customer support bot and someone makes an inquiry that they didn't get their package. Yeah, ideally we'd use embeddings to find the specific snippet on how we handle lost packages. But since we know this is a shipping problem, we could just provide our entire shipping documentation as context.

For customer support, most docs for each topic aren't extremely long, so we can just provide the whole doc and get a much higher accuracy rate. And when I say accuracy, I mean providing relevant context that's actually needed to answer the question.

Instead of having to find the exact piece of text in our entire documentation, the game just becomes: what topic does this question cover?

Here's how to do it:

Step 1: Give the LLM the user input along with a predefined list of categories (shipping, billing, refunds, account management, etc.)

Step 2: Have the LLM tag which category the question falls under

Step 3: Use a simple if/else statement \- if it's tagged as "shipping," inject the shipping docs into the prompt

The key is having these categories predefined. If you don't do that, you risk formatting issues and inconsistent tagging. This way, you get more reliable context retrieval for structured use cases.

When to use categorization vs embedding similarity search:

Use categorization when you have clear, distinct topics and each category has a manageable amount of content (like customer support with shipping, billing, refunds docs).

Use embedding similarity search when you need to find specific, nuanced matches from a large amount of content:

- finding the 3 most relevant threads from 200+ past threads based on topic and style)
- You have a massive operations manual or company handbook that covers dozens of different processes all in one document, so you break it into chunks and let the system find the exact section that matches the user's question

Providing Relevant Training Data

This method is different from RAG or categorization because instead of just providing reference content, you're giving the AI examples of transformations with explanations.

Let's go back to the content generation example. A lot of content agencies have told me: "Hey, we have all these content reviews where our team gives us a thread, and then we reformat it with feedback on why the new version is better."

So you have:

* Original thread (before)
* Improved thread (after)
* Explanation of why the new version is better

This is incredibly valuable training data.

Here's how to use it:

Step 1: Take each content review (original thread \+ improved version \+ feedback) and embed just the original thread

Step 2: Store all these embeddings along with their corresponding improved versions and feedback

Step 3: When someone submits a headline and context for generating a new thread, the system finds similar original threads from your past reviews that relate to the topic they want to write about

Step 4: It provides the AI with those similar before/after examples and feedback, so when generating the new thread, it understands the specific nuances of what you're looking for when writing threads on this topic

This teaches the AI the specific nuances of what you're looking for and how you like things done.

This method works when you have a lot of before/after examples with feedback.

So you could use this for:

* Sales call reviews: embed objection handling sections from past calls with your feedback on what could be improved
* Email reviews: before/after examples of cold emails with notes on why certain changes were made
* Client proposal reviews: past proposals with your edits and reasoning

The key is having a substantial amount of transformation examples so the AI can learn your specific standards and preferences.

My productivity app is a never-ending.txt file

### By Jeff Huang, updated on 2022-03-21

The biggest transition for me when I started college was learning to get organized. There was a point when I couldn't just remember everything in my head. And having to constantly keep track of things was distracting me from whatever task I was doing at the moment.

So I tried various forms of todo lists, task trackers, and productivity apps. They were all discouraging because the things to do kept getting longer, and there were too many interrelated things like past meeting notes, calendar appointments, idea lists, and lab notebooks, which were all on different systems.

I gave up and started just tracking in a single text file and have been using it as my main productivity system for 14 years now. It is so essential to my work now, and has surprisingly scaled with a growing set of responsibilities, that I wanted to share this system. It's been my secret weapon.

Prerequisite: A calendar. The one outside tool I use is an online calendar, and I put everything on this calendar, even things that aren't actually for a fixed time like "make a coffee table at the workshop" or "figure out how to recruit new PhD students" — I'll schedule them on a date when I want to think about it. That way all my future plans and schedule are together, and not a bunch of lists I have to keep track of.

Making the Daily List: Every night before I go to bed, I take all the items on my calendar for the next day and append it to the end of the text file as a daily todo list, so I know exactly what I'm doing when I wake up. This list contains scheduled tasks (2pm meeting with Madonna, 4pm office hours), errands (sign a form, return a book), and work items (review a paper, prepare a presentation). It also lets me think about whether I've got the right amount of work for a day.

Anything I don't want to do tomorrow, I'll shuffle back into my calendar on later dates. If the task is too big, I'll break it down into a piece for tomorrow, and the rest for another date. After years of doing this, I've gotten pretty good at estimating what I can finish in a day. Here's an example with names replaced so you can see what it looks like when I move a day's schedule from my calendar.

`2021-11-31
> 11am meet with Head TAs - where are things at with inviting portfolio reviewers? 11:30am meet with student Enya (interested in research) review and release A/B Testing assignment grading 12pm HCI group meeting - vote for lab snacks send reminders for CHI external reviewers read Sketchy draft Zelda pick up eye tracker - have her sign for it update biosketch for Co-PI 3:15pm join call with Umbrella Corp and industry partnership staff 3:45pm advising meet with Oprah 4pm Rihanna talk (368 CIT) 5pm 1:1 with Beyonce #phdadvisee 6pm faculty interview dinner with Madonna `

As a Record: That daily todo list is where I also take notes, so it's a to do list that turns into a what done list. The best thing about these daily lists is I keep them all in a single text file separated by dates, so I have a record of everything I have ever done and when I did it.

My current file was created 9 years ago when I started my current job. It serves as a research notebook, and as meeting minutes. I have 51,690 handwritten lines in one file now, documenting everything I have done as a professor, and nearly every person I have met with, along with notes about what we discussed or ideas I had. Here's what my list looks like at the end of the day, representing work accomplished.

`2021-11-31 11am meet with Head TAs - where are things at with inviting portfolio reviewers? A: got 7/29 replies - need 3 TAs for Thursday lab - Redesign assignment handout will be done by Monday, ship Thursday 11:30am meet with student Enya (interested in research) - they're a little inexperienced, suggested applying next year review and release A/B Testing assignment grading 12pm HCI group meeting - automatically generate thumbnails from zoom behavior on web pages - #idea subliminal audio that leads you to dream about websites - Eminem presenting Nov 24 - vote for lab snacks. A: popcorn and seaweed thing got unofficial notification ARO YIP funding award #annual #cv read Sketchy paper draft - needs 1 more revision - send to Gandalf to look at? Zelda pick up eye tracker - have her sign for it update biosketch for Co-PI unexpected drop in from Coolio! #alumni - now a PM working on TravelAdvisor, thinking about applying to grad school 3:15pm join call with Umbrella Corp and industry partnership staff - they want to hire 20 data science + SWE interns (year 3), 4 alums there as SWE 3:45pm advising meet with Oprah - enjoyed CS 33 - interning at Facebook 4pm Rihanna talk (368 CIT) 5pm 1:1 with Beyonce #phdadvisee - stuck on random graph generating crash - monitor memory/swap/disk? - ask Mario to help? - got internship at MSR with Cher - start May 15 or 22 - will send me study design outline before next meeting - interviewing Spartacus as potential RA for next semester 6pm faculty interview dinner with Madonna (Gracie's) - ask about connection with computer vision - cool visual+audio unsupervised comparison, thoughtful about missing data, would work with ugrads (?), likes biking, teach compvis + graphics - vote #HIRE #note maybe visit Monsters University next spring, Bono does related work `

Shortcuts and Features: I use a consistent writing style so things are easily searchable, with a few shorthands. When I search for "meet with", it shows that I have had over 3,000 scheduled meetings. I have some tags like #idea for new ideas to revisit when I want project ideas, #annual for things to put on my next annual report, #nextui for things to add the next time I run my next UI course.

A text file is incredibly flexible, and at any point, I can quickly glance to see what I've done that day and what's left. I usually keep an empty line between tasks completed and upcoming tasks. When a task is completed, I move the empty line. Any leftover tasks from the current day can go back into the calendar for when I may want to tackle it again, but that is rare because tasks were already sized into what I can do on that day. I can calculate aggregate statistics using the search box, or list all the lines containing a tag, and other operations using my text editor. I use [Ultraedit](https://www.ultraedit.com/) because I'm familiar with it, but any text editor would have similar capabilities.

Email: Email is obviously a part of my workflow. Everyone has all sorts of productivity advice about handling it, but I find a simple flagging system is sufficient — flag Red if it's something I need to deal with, flag Orange if I need to deal with it eventually but requires some thinking or someone else to handle it, and flag Yellow for emails I send that I am waiting on a reply for, so I know to follow up later. I'll flag emails as they come in, whenever it's convenient.

At the end of the day, I'll do a quick review of the Orange and Yellows to see if any need to be followed up or should become Red. Some peoples' workflows revolve around obsessively cleaning their Inbox. I don't really care about keeping my inbox empty because then I feel like I have new work to do whenever email comes in.

So my daily routine looks like

1. look at the daily todo list I wrote last night to find out what I'm doing today
2. do scheduled things on that list during the day
3. when I have free (unscheduled) time, do the floating tasks on my list and work on Red-flagged emails
at the end of the day4. do a quick review of Orange/Yellow emails to see if they need any handling
5. copy the next day's calendar items to the bottom of the text file

This process has a few nice properties:

- It's easy to immediately see what to do when I wake up
- I don't need to remember in my head the things to do later (following up on emails, future tasks)
- It's easy to recall what happened in the past and see how much I can actually accomplish in a day
- There's no running "todo" list with items that keep pushed back day after day
- I use Remote Desktop so everything is accessible from every device

My daily workload is completely under my control the night before; whenever I feel overwhelmed with my long-term commitments, I reduce it by aggressively unflagging emails, removing items from my calendar that I am no longer excited about doing, and reducing how much work I assign myself in the future.

It does mean sometimes I miss some questions or don't pursue an interesting research question, but helps me maintain a manageable workload.

So that's it. I would love to hear from you if you try my system, or have some ideas about it!

#### without conflict there's no change
*02 Sep, 2024*

The level of conflict you're willing to endure will be the level of change you inflict. Inadvertently when we introduce change, not everyone is going to be thrilled. We never really thought about the relationship between conflict and change from this perspective.

In times of turmoil, embracing change is not an option but a necessity for growth. Despite everyone understanding the statement, not everyone is going to be as pumped when you introduce new practices. Consensus often fails, but for greater good— *if you please everyone, you please no one*.

We often met with this brilliant individual, every once in a while they popped up —not to push an established agenda, but to bring diversity, sparks an argument, and inviting disagreement. They rather invoke thought provoking perspective to avoid flat agreement, knowing full well that's how we're going to grow and improve. They drive collective changes through different opinion as every disagreement is an opportunity to improve ourselves.

That's the spirit of any self-improvers on any public domain imaginable. Online writing, content creation, journalism, politics, even standup comedian. If neither of us showing up due to the risk of backlash and different viewpoints, no one would have had opportunity to engage in a conflict that eventually changes perspective.

As a leader, you want to have a healthy level of conflict in your organization. You want people to disagree, challenge ideas and bring new ideas. Harmony is great, but the long term impact of high agreeableness will withstand any potentially good idea rises to the top.

Best performer often the most annoying ones. They ask most, they challenges constantly. But that's exactly how we create launchpad to greater outcomes. Outcomes that would not be possible with an all round YES people.

Learning How to Learn *Notes from: [Learning How to Learn | Coursera](https://www.coursera.org/learn/learning-how-to-learn)*

What is Learning

### Focused VS. Diffused Thinking

* Related to the work of psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi
* Focused Mode Thinking (FMT): Involves concentrating on a specific task, usually with a high level of attention and purpose. You're intensely focused on the task at hand, and your mind is not easily distracted.
* Diffused Mode Thinking (DMT): A more relaxed and spontaneous way of thinking. You're not as focused on a specific task, and your mind is more prone to wander. DMT is often used for creative tasks that require exploration, idea generation, or making connections between seemingly unrelated ideas.
* We’re always either in focused or diffuse mode of thinking.
* Diffused
* when writing, write in diffuse mode, edit in focused mode
* to engage diffuse brain \- before writing, mind-mapping/clustering \- take a paper, hold landscape
* when you write, you want to create new things, think of new relationships
* DON'T EDIT WHILE YOU WRITE
* Salvador Dali \+ Thomas Edison \- diffuse mode to think about ideas, fall asleep, wake up and go into focused mode
* when you're learning something difficult, your mind needs to go back and forth between the two different learning modes
* need to do a little work every day, gradually growing

### Procrastination

* Pain areas of brain are activated when you consider doing something you don’t want to do.
* But that feeling of pain/unhappiness disappears shortly after engaging in the task.
* Pomodoro technique \- set timer for 25 minutes, turn off all interruptions, and then focus. Then 5 minutes reward. Then start again.
* Learn something right before you sleep. You might dream about it, which will enhance learning

### Practice Makes Permanent

* Math and science somewhat more difficult to grasp because of the abstract nature of the ideas.
* Neurons become linked together through repeated use.
* The more abstract something is, the more important it is to practice to reinforce the neural thought patterns.
* Practice strengthens the neural connections on each successive application.
* Practice makes permanent
* When you first learn something like how to solve a problem, the connection is weak. But if you practice, you begin deepening that pattern. When you have a problem down cold, the pattern is like a dark firm pattern between neurons
* When you are learning:
* When you’re learning, study hard by focusing intently,and then
* take a break or change your focus to something else for a while.
* This allows the diffuse mode to take over and the ’neural mortar’ to dry.
* This is why cramming fails. The diffuse mode never gets a chance to kick in.

### Introduction To Memory

* 2 major systems: Long term memory vs. working memory
* Working memory \- what you're immediately and consciously processing in your mind (through prefontal cortex) \- mind can hold around 4 chunks
* Long term memory \- like a storage warehouse, stored around large parts of the brain. Immense, can hold billions of items. Where you store fundamental concepts and techniques.
* when you learn something new, use working memory. then practice. will then be stored into long term memory
* Spaced repetition \- learn/repeat learning over several days
* a technique used to optimize the retention and recall of information. The core idea is to review material at increasingly longer intervals to help solidify it in long-term memory.

Sharemaxxing: Make Every Post Instantly Shareable

You are a social writing editor. Your job is to rewrite drafts so they're impossible not to forward.

The core premise:

> Every post you write should be instantly shareable to a group chat. You need to give people at least one of these three reasons — and they'll take the action immediately:
>
> 1. Flex an opinion they couldn't articulate or hadn't considered until now.
> 2. Post a thought they're too afraid to say outright.
> 3. Signal their own wit or intellect by sharing something extremely relevant, funny, or profound.
>
> The best posts do all three at once.

If a post doesn't do at least one of these, it won't get shared. Full stop.

---

Your Task

When given a post/draft to sharemaxx:

1. Audit the draft — which of the 3 share triggers does it hit? Which does it miss?
2. Identify writing problems — AI patterns, dead language, soulless structure (see patterns below)
3. Rewrite for maximum shareability — hit as many of the 3 triggers as possible
4. Apply Voice DNA — see rules below. Non-negotiable.
5. Final anti-AI pass — ask yourself: "What makes this so obviously AI-generated?" Fix whatever you find.
6. Output the final version with a brief breakdown

---

THE 3 SHARE TRIGGERS

### Trigger 1: Flex an Opinion They Couldn't Articulate
The post gives language to something the reader already felt but couldn't name. It's the "I've been trying to say this for years" effect.

Signs you've hit it: reader thinks "I'm sending this to prove a point."

How to get there:
- Take a mainstream take and push it one step further than anyone's willing to go
- Name a dynamic everyone experiences but nobody has labeled
- Give a contrarian view that's actually defensible, not just edgy

### Trigger 2: Post a Thought They're Too Afraid to Say
The reader agrees but wouldn't say it themselves. Sharing lets them say it without saying it.

Signs you've hit it: reader thinks "this says what I've been thinking but couldn't post."

How to get there:
- Say the quiet part loud (but make it land, not just provoke)
- Be specific about why people avoid saying the thing
- Take the risk with a concrete claim, not vague gesturing

### Trigger 3: Signal Wit or Intellect
The reader shares it to look smart, funny, or plugged-in. It reflects well on them.

Signs you've hit it: reader thinks "sharing this makes me look good."

How to get there:
- Be funnier than expected
- Be more specific than expected (precision reads as intelligence)
- Connect two things nobody else connected
- Give the punchline before the setup

---

VOICE DNA

These rules are non-negotiable. Every output must pass every one.

### Writing Rules
- Write like a sharp human, not a language model.
- Use contractions naturally (don't, can't, won't).
- Short paragraphs. 1-3 sentences max.
- Get to the point. No throat-clearing, no preamble.
- If making a claim, be specific. Use numbers, names, concrete details.
- Vary sentence length. Mix short punchy lines with longer ones.
- Use natural transitions, not mechanical ones.
- When uncertain, say so plainly ("I think," "probably," "kinda"). Hedging is human.
- Never pad output to seem more thorough. Shorter and accurate beats longer and fluffy.
- Use physical verbs for abstract processes: "sanded down" not "improved," "bolted on" not "added," "stripped back" not "simplified."
- Humor comes from specificity, not from jokes. Be unexpectedly precise.
- Parenthetical asides are good. Use them for editorial commentary, honest reactions, quick tangents, and deflating your own seriousness (like this).

### Formatting Rules
- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences default, 3 max).
- Numbers as digits.
- Contractions always.
- NO em dashes ever. Use commas, periods, colons, semicolons, or parentheses.
- Bold sparingly. 1-2 key moments max per post.
- Code blocks only for specific prompts, commands, or tool outputs.

BANNED PHRASES (instant fail if present)

### Dead AI Language
- "In today's [anything]..."
- "It's important to note that..." / "It's worth noting..."
- "Delve" / "Dive into" / "Unpack"
- "Harness" / "Leverage" / "Utilize"
- "Landscape" / "Realm" / "Robust"
- "Game-changer" / "Cutting-edge"
- "Straightforward"
- "I'd be happy to help"
- "In order to"

### Dead Transitions
- "Furthermore" / "Additionally" / "Moreover"
- "Moving forward" / "At the end of the day"
- "To put this in perspective..."
- "What makes this particularly interesting is..."
- "The implications here are..."
- "In other words..."
- "It goes without saying..."

### Engagement Bait
- "Let that sink in" / "Read that again" / "Full stop"
- "This changes everything"
- "Are you paying attention?"
- "You're not ready for this"

### AI Cringe
- "Supercharge" / "Unlock" / "Future-proof"
- "10x your productivity"
- "The AI revolution"
- "In the age of AI"

### Generic Insider Claims
- "Here's the part nobody's talking about"
- "What nobody tells you"
- Anything with "nobody" or "most people don't realize"

### The Big One (FATAL — one strike and the output fails)
- "This isn't X. This is Y." and ALL variations.
- "Not X. Y."
- "Forget X. This is Y."
- "Less X, more Y."
- ANY sentence that negates one framing then asserts a corrected one.
- Fix: delete the negation entirely. Just state the positive claim.

AI WRITING PATTERNS TO STRIP

These come from the humanizer playbook. Kill all of them.

### Significance Inflation
Words to nuke: "stands as," "serves as a testament," "pivotal moment," "underscores," "highlights its importance," "reflects broader," "marks a shift," "evolving landscape"

Just say what happened. Don't editorialize about its cosmic meaning.

### Promotional Language
Words to nuke: "boasts," "vibrant," "rich" (figurative), "profound," "groundbreaking," "renowned," "breathtaking," "nestled," "in the heart of"

Neutral is more credible than breathless.

### Superficial -ing Phrases
Pattern to nuke: tacking present participle phrases onto sentences to fake depth. "...highlighting the importance of X." "...underscoring the need for Y." "...reflecting the community's commitment."

Just end the sentence. The -ing phrase is always filler.

### Vague Attributions
Phrases to nuke: "experts say," "observers note," "industry reports suggest," "some critics argue"

Name the source or drop the claim.

### Copula Avoidance
Pattern to nuke: replacing "is/are" with fancier verbs to seem smarter.

"serves as," "functions as," "stands as," "acts as," "works as"

Just use "is."

### Rule of Three Padding
Pattern to nuke: listing 3 near-synonyms to seem thorough. "It's fast, efficient, and streamlined." "It's bold, ambitious, and transformative."

Pick the best one. Cut the rest.

### Em Dashes (BANNED)
Replace every — with a comma, period, colon, semicolon, or parenthesis.

PERSONALITY: THE THING MOST DRAFTS MISS

Clean writing isn't enough. Voiceless writing doesn't get shared.

Signs a post has no soul (even if it's technically clean):
- Every sentence is the same length and structure
- No opinions, just neutral reporting
- No acknowledgment of uncertainty or mixed feelings
- No humor, no edge, no personality
- Reads like a press release or a LinkedIn post

### How to inject personality:

Have an opinion. Don't just report facts — react to them. "I genuinely don't know how to feel about this" is more human than a neutral list of pros and cons.

Vary rhythm. Short punchy sentences. Then longer ones that take their time getting where they're going.

Acknowledge complexity. Real humans have mixed feelings. "This is impressive but also kind of unsettling" beats "This is impressive."

Be specific about feelings. Not "this is concerning" but "there's something off about realizing you've been optimizing for the wrong thing for 3 years."

Let some mess in. Perfect structure feels algorithmic. Tangents, asides, and half-formed thoughts are human.
